Measures of Location / Central Tendency

Arithmetic Mean (Average)

Definition: The median is the middle value in an ordered dataset. When all observations are arranged from smallest to largest, the median divides the dataset into two equal halves‚Äî50% of observations lie below it and 50% above it. For an even number of observations, it is calculated as the average of the two central values. Mode

Definition: The mode is the most frequently occurring value in a dataset. It represents the data point or category that appears with the greatest frequency. In distributions with one clear peak (unimodal), it indicates the most common observation. When multiple peaks exist, the dataset may be bimodal or multimodal, reflecting multiple groups or patterns within the data.

Use: The only central measure meaningful for nominal (categorical) data; also useful to identify peaks in multimodal distributions.

Advantages: Simple and interpretable for categories; can be used on any data type. Limitations: May be non-unique (bimodal, multimodal) or not informative for continuous data where exact repeats are rare.

Trimmed Mean & Winsorized Mean

Definition: A trimmed mean is computed after discarding a fixed proportion of the smallest and largest observations before averaging, reducing the influence of outliers. A winsorized mean replaces those extreme values with the nearest retained ones rather than removing them. Both methods aim to estimate a stable central value that represents the majority of the dataset, while maintaining robustness to anomalies.

Use:

Advantages: Reduces influence of extremes while retaining more data than the median. Limitations: Requires choosing trimming/winsorizing cutoffs; less standard for inference unless explicitly accounted for.

Weighted Mean

Definition: The weighted mean assigns different levels of importance or frequency to each observation. Each value x_i is multiplied by its corresponding weight w_i, and the sum of weighted values is divided by the sum of the weights. Mid-range

Definition: The mid-range is the arithmetic mean of the maximum and minimum values in a dataset. It represents the midpoint of the entire range of data values. Although it provides a rough estimate of the central location, it can be distorted by extreme outliers because it only considers the two extremes rather than the full distribution of observations.

(min + max) / 2

Use: Quick summary of central location for symmetric distributions in exploratory analysis. Advantages: Very easy to compute. Limitations: Extremely sensitive to outliers; ignores all interior data.

Measures of Dispersion / Variability

Range

Definition: The range measures the total spread of a dataset by subtracting the smallest observation from the largest. It provides a quick and simple sense of the total variation but depends entirely on only two data points. Because of this, it is highly affected by outliers and does not describe how values are distributed between the extremes.

Use: Quick, rough measure of total spread. Advantages: Simple and intuitive. Limitations: Depends only on two values; highly sensitive to outliers; ignores internal distribution.

Interquartile Range (IQR)

Definition: The interquartile range quantifies the spread of the middle 50% of a dataset. It is computed as the difference between the 75th percentile (Q3) and the 25th percentile (Q1). This measure effectively eliminates the influence of outliers and extreme values, showing how concentrated or dispersed the core of the data distribution is.

IQR = Q3 - Q1

Use: Measures spread of the middle 50% of data; used in boxplots and robust summaries. Advantages: Resistant to extreme values and skew. Limitations: Ignores tails; not always suited for parametric methods relying on variance.
